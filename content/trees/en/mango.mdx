## Physical Description

### Overall Form

The Mango is a large, long-lived evergreen tree with a dense, rounded crown. Mature trees are impressiveâ€”broad-canopied, heavily branched, and often dominant in the landscape. The tree can live for over 100 years, with some specimens producing fruit for centuries. Young growth is often bronze or reddish, creating seasonal color contrasts.

### Distinctive Features

<TwoColumn>
<Column>

#### Leaves

- **Type**: Simple, lanceolate
- **Size**: 15-35 cm long
- **Color**: Dark green (bronze when young)
- **Texture**: Leathery, glossy
- **Arrangement**: Alternate, clustered
- **Evergreen**: Yes, but periodic leaf drop

#### Bark

- **Color**: Gray to dark brown
- **Texture**: Rough, fissured
- **Pattern**: Scaly with age
- **Feature**: Exudes resin when damaged

</Column>
<Column>

#### The Glorious Fruit

- **Shape**: Oval to kidney-shaped
- **Size**: 5-25 cm (varies by variety)
- **Skin**: Green, yellow, red, or orange
- **Flesh**: Yellow to orange, fibrous to smooth
- **Stone**: Large, flat, fibrous
- **Season**: Mainly March-July in Costa Rica
- **Yield**: 100-400+ fruits per tree

#### Flowers

- **Type**: Small, numerous in panicles
- **Color**: Pinkish-white to yellowish
- **Size**: Panicle 15-30 cm long
- **Number**: 500-6,000 flowers per panicle!
- **Fragrance**: Sweet, sometimes strong

</Column>
</TwoColumn>

<Callout type="tip" title="The Perfect Mango">
  Choosing and eating a perfect mango: **Signs of Ripeness**: - Gives slightly
  to gentle pressure - Sweet aroma at stem end - Color change (varies by
  variety) - Some varieties never change color much **Eating Methods**: - The
  "hedgehog" cut (cross-hatch and invert) - Peeled and sliced - Eating straight
  off the seed (messy but fun!) - Blended into smoothies **The Debate**: Should
  mangoes be refrigerated? Some say yes (for firmness), purists say never (dulls
  flavor). Try both!
</Callout>

## History and Spread

### From India to the World

<FeatureBox title="4,000 Years of Cultivation" icon="ðŸŒ">
  Mango has one of the longest cultivation histories: **Ancient Origins**: -
  Domesticated in South Asia ~4,000 years ago - Sacred in Hindu and Buddhist
  traditions - Featured in ancient Indian literature - First fruit trees to be
  cultivated **Colonial Spread**: - Portuguese brought to Africa (1500s) -
  Spread to Brazil (1700s) - Reached Caribbean and Central America (1700s-1800s)
  - Arrived in Costa Rica in colonial period **Modern Era**: - Now grown in 100+
  countries - India produces 50% of world's mangoes - Second most traded
  tropical fruit - Over 1,000 named varieties worldwide
</FeatureBox>

### Arrival in Costa Rica

<Callout type="info" title="Mangoes in Costa Rica">
  Though not native, mangoes are completely integrated into Costa Rican life: -
  Introduced during Spanish colonial period - Now found in nearly every town and
  farm - Many naturalized seedling trees - Important commercial production -
  Essential part of seasonal rhythms "Mango season" (temporada de mango) is a
  real eventâ€”when trees are laden with fruit, people collect them, share them,
  and celebrate the abundance!
</Callout>

## Cultivation

### Growing Mangoes

<Accordion>
  <AccordionItem title="Site Selection">
    **Ideal Conditions**: - Full sun (essential) - Protected from strong winds -
    Well-drained soil - Low elevation (below 600m) - Distinct dry season
    (promotes flowering) **Avoid**: - Waterlogged sites - Heavy shade -
    Frost-prone areas - Very high rainfall (reduces flowering)
  </AccordionItem>

<AccordionItem title="Propagation">
  **From Seed**: - Polyembryonic seeds produce true-to-type - Monoembryonic
  seeds variable - 5-8 years to fruit - Large, vigorous trees **Grafting**
  (preferred commercial): - Ensures variety characteristics - Fruit in 3-4 years
  - Smaller, manageable trees - Air layering also used
</AccordionItem>

<AccordionItem title="Care and Harvest">
  **Maintenance**: - Prune after harvest to maintain size - Fertilize 2-3 times
  per year - Irrigation critical during flowering - Pest management for fruit
  flies **Harvest**: - Pick when mature (shoulder development) - Handle
  carefully (bruises easily) - Ripen at room temperature - Commercial: picked
  green, ripened later
</AccordionItem>

  <AccordionItem title="Companion Planting">
    **Beneficial Companions**:
    - **Leguminous cover crops (Mucuna, Desmodium)**: Fix nitrogen, suppress weeds, improve soil fertility
    - **Turmeric & ginger**: Groundcover that retains moisture and provides secondary income
    - **Lemongrass & citronella**: Natural pest repellents help deter fruit flies and other insects
    - **Marigolds (_Tagetes_)**: Attract beneficial insects, repel some pests
    - **Pigeon peas**: Nitrogen fixation, windbreak for young trees, edible crop
    - **Sweet potato**: Living mulch, prevents erosion on slopes

    **Compatible Fruit Trees** (maintain 10-15m spacing):
    - Citrus (oranges, limes) - similar water needs, lower stature
    - Avocado - complementary harvest seasons
    - Sapote (_Pouteria sapota_) - similar cultural requirements
    - Mamey - compatible growth habits
    - Coconut palms - provide light filtered shade when young

    **Plants to Avoid**:
    - Other large canopy trees within 15m (competition for light, water, nutrients)
    - Water-demanding vegetables directly under canopy (mango monopolizes water)
    - Shallow-rooted crops near trunk (roots extend far beyond canopy)
    - Heavy shade-producing plants that reduce flowering

    **Agroforestry Integration**:
    - Excellent as scattered overstory tree in pasture systems
    - Compatible with shade-tolerant crops (cacao, vanilla) when young
    - Works well in home orchard polyculture with adequate spacing
    - Traditional "mango + citrus + livestock" systems common in Guanacaste
    - Living fence integration (plant 10-15m apart along property lines)
    - Suitable for silvopastoral systems (cattle can graze under mature trees)

  </AccordionItem>

  <AccordionItem title="Seasonal Care Calendar">
    **December-February (Dry Season - Flowering Period)**:
    - **CRITICAL**: Reduce or stop watering 8-12 weeks before expected flowering (November) to stress tree and trigger bloom
    - Monitor flower panicle development (usually December-January)
    - Apply pre-flowering fertilizer in November (low nitrogen, high phosphorus: 5-30-15)
    - Begin irrigation once flowers open to support fruit set
    - Hand-pollinate if bee activity is low
    - Scout for and control powdery mildew on flowers (sulfur dust if needed)
    - Monitor for anthracnose on developing panicles

    **March-April (Early Rainy Season - Fruit Development)**:
    - Peak fruit development period
    - Maintain consistent watering (critical for fruit sizing)
    - Apply balanced fertilizer (15-15-15) in March
    - Thin fruit clusters if excessive (improves fruit size and reduces alternate bearing)
    - Install fruit fly traps (methyl eugenol or protein baits)
    - Bag developing fruits for premium quality (paper bags prevent fruit flies and sunburn)
    - Watch for fruit drop (normal; tree self-thins)

    **May-August (Rainy Season - Harvest & Post-Harvest)**:
    - Main harvest period (May-July depending on variety)
    - Harvest when fruit develops "shoulders" and skin color lightens
    - Pick fruit before fully ripe (will ripen in 3-7 days at room temperature)
    - **Post-harvest pruning** (August): Remove diseased wood, thin canopy, manage height
    - Reduce or stop fertilization during heavy rains (leaching risk)
    - Monitor for anthracnose on fruit and leaves (copper fungicide if severe)
    - Collect and destroy fallen fruit to reduce pest populations
    - Apply final annual fertilizer in late August (after pruning)

    **September-November (Late Rainy/Transition - Vegetative Growth)**:
    - Trees put on vegetative growth
    - Reduce watering in preparation for dry season flowering stress
    - No fertilization (encourages vegetative growth over flower formation)
    - Clean up fallen leaves and fruit (sanitation reduces pests/diseases)
    - Scout for and treat any scale, mealybug, or sooty mold issues
    - November: Begin withholding water to induce flowering stress

  </AccordionItem>
</Accordion>
