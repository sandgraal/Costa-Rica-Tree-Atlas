# Sotacaballo (Riverside Mimosa)

<Callout type="success" title="Guardian of the Riverbanks">
  The **Sotacaballo** (_Zygia longifolia_) is one of the most characteristic
  trees of Costa Rica's waterways. Its distinctive habit of leaning dramatically
  over rivers and streams provides essential shade for aquatic life while its
  roots stabilize banks against erosion. The powder-puff flowers are a favorite
  of bees and hummingbirds.
</Callout>

## Physical Description

### Overall Form

The Sotacaballo is a small to medium evergreen tree with a distinctive growth habit. It almost always leans toward water, often at dramatic angles, with its branches sweeping over the river surface. The trunk frequently forks low, creating multiple stems that arch gracefully over waterways.

## Interesting Facts

<Accordion>
<AccordionItem title="ðŸŒŠ Why the Lean?">

The dramatic lean of Sotacaballo toward water isn't accidentalâ€”it's an adaptation
for light competition. By growing over the open space of rivers and streams, the
tree accesses full sunlight that would otherwise be blocked by taller forest
trees. This phototropic response shapes the tree's distinctive silhouette.

</AccordionItem>
<AccordionItem title="ðŸ Bee Highway">

Sotacaballo trees flowering along rivers create a "nectar highway" for native
bees. Beekeepers in Costa Rica value areas with healthy Sotacaballo populations
because the trees provide reliable forage during the dry-to-wet season
transition when other food sources are scarce.

</AccordionItem>
<AccordionItem title="ðŸŸ Fish Friend">

The shade provided by Sotacaballo is critical for stream fish. Research shows
that water temperatures under these trees can be 2-4Â°C cooler than in exposed
reachesâ€”a difference that can mean survival for cold-water fish during dry
season low flows.

</AccordionItem>
<AccordionItem title="ðŸ’§ Natural Engineers">

The root systems of Sotacaballo act as natural bioengineering structures.
During floods, the flexible roots bend rather than break, dissipating water
energy while holding soil in place. This makes the species invaluable for
natural river management approaches.
