### Distinctive Features

<TwoColumn>
<Column>

#### Leaves

- **Type**: Pinnately compound
- **Leaflets**: 10-40 pairs, small
- **Size**: Leaflets 1-2.5 cm each
- **Texture**: Delicate, feathery
- **Behavior**: Close at night (nyctinasty)
- **Evergreen**: In wet climates; deciduous in dry

#### Bark

- **Color**: Dark gray to black
- **Texture**: Deeply fissured, rough
- **Pattern**: Scaly, rugged
- **Appearance**: Ancient-looking even when young

</Column>
<Column>

#### The Tangy Pods

- **Shape**: Elongated, curved, lumpy
- **Length**: 7-20 cm
- **Shell**: Brittle brown when ripe
- **Pulp**: Sticky, brown, fibrous
- **Seeds**: 4-12 per pod, hard, shiny
- **Flavor**: Sweet-sour, complex

#### Flowers

- **Type**: Small, inconspicuous
- **Color**: Yellow with red veins
- **Size**: 2.5 cm across
- **Arrangement**: Few-flowered racemes
- **Timing**: Dry season

</Column>
</TwoColumn>

  finish**: Refreshing and moreish This unique flavor profile makes tamarind

  America) - Worcestershire sauce - Indian chutneys and sambar - Candies

<Callout type="tip" title="The Classic Costa Rican Drink">
  Making traditional "agua de tamarindo": **Ingredients**: - Tamarind pods
  (shelled) - Water - Sugar to taste **Method**: 1. Soak tamarind pulp in warm
  water 2. Mash and strain to remove seeds/fibers 3. Add cold water to dilute 4.
  Sweeten to taste 5. Serve very cold, often with ice **Tips**: - Adjust
  sweetness to balance sourness - Can add lime for extra tang - Best made fresh
  - Perfect for hot days! In Costa Rica, this is one of the most refreshing
  "refrescos naturales."

## Cultivation

### Growing Tamarind

<Accordion>
  <AccordionItem title="Site Requirements">
    **Ideal Conditions**: - Full sun (essential) - Deep, well-drained soil -
    Hot, dry climate preferred - Space for massive growth - Drought-tolerant
    once established **Consider**: - Trees get VERY large - Deep taproot -
    Long-lived (plan for generations) - Slow initial growth
  </AccordionItem>

<AccordionItem title="Propagation">
  **From Seed**: - Scarify or soak seeds - Germination in 1-2 weeks - Variable
  fruit quality - Trees fruit in 6-10 years **Vegetative** (commercial): -
  Grafting for superior varieties - Air layering - Ensures fruit quality -
  Fruits faster (3-4 years)
</AccordionItem>

  <AccordionItem title="Care and Harvest">
    **Watering Requirements**:
    - **Young trees (Years 1-3)**: Water weekly during first two dry seasons
    - **Established trees**: Extremely drought-tolerantâ€”survives on seasonal rainfall alone
    - After year 3-5, natural rainfall usually sufficient even in Guanacaste dry zone
    - One of the most water-efficient fruit/shade trees
    - Overwatering not beneficialâ€”adapted to dry forest conditions
    - Deep watering during extreme drought can improve fruit quality

    **Fertilization Schedule**:
    - **Generally NOT NEEDED**: Tamarind is nitrogen-fixing legume
    - Young trees may benefit from light balanced fertilizer if growing in very poor soil
    - Mature trees thrive without fertilization
    - Tree improves its own soil through nitrogen fixation
    - Compost mulch beneficial but not essential
    - **Note**: One of the lowest-maintenance fruit trees for fertilization

    **Pruning Guidelines**:
    - **Minimal pruning required**
    - Young tree training: Allow natural spreading form to develop
    - Remove dead, damaged, or crossing branches as needed
    - Can lightly prune to raise canopy if needed for access
    - Mature trees rarely need pruning
    - Best time: Late dry season before new growth
    - Large trees may shed branches naturallyâ€”clear fallen limbs

    **Pest & Disease Management**:
    - **Exceptionally pest/disease-free**
    - Scale insects: Occasional issueâ€”usually not requiring treatment
    - Virtually no serious pest problems
    - Borers in very stressed treesâ€”keep tree healthy
    - One of the most trouble-free tropical trees

    **Harvest Guidelines**:
    - Pods ready December-March (main dry season)
    - Mature pods turn brown and shell becomes brittle
    - Crack open pods to extract sticky pulp and seeds
    - Can be harvested over extended periodâ€”pods don't spoil on tree
    - Yields increase with age: 100-300+ kg per mature tree
    - Pods store wellâ€”can be dried for long-term storage
    - Process pulp by removing seeds, can freeze or dry for later use

  </AccordionItem>

  <AccordionItem title="Companion Planting">
    **Beneficial Companions**:
    - **Shade-tolerant crops under canopy**: Ginger, turmeric, shade coffee (light filtered shade)
    - **Pasture integration**: Excellent scattered shade tree in silvopastureâ€”improves forage quality
    - **Annual crops while young**: Beans, squash, melons between young trees
    - **Other nitrogen-fixers**: Compatible with other legume trees

    **Compatible Trees**:
    - Other drought-tolerant species:
      - Neem (Azadirachta indica)
      - Mango (full sun required)
      - Cashew (compatible spacing)
      - Moringa (light filtered shade tolerant)
      - Native dry forest species

    **Plants to Avoid**:
    - High-water requirement crops (tamarind creates dry microclimate)
    - Shallow-rooted plants competing with tamarind's deep taproot
    - Shade-intolerant crops directly under mature canopy

    **Agroforestry Applications**:
    - **Silvopasture**: Premier shade tree for cattleâ€”improves forage, provides shelter
    - **Living fence**: Can be planted as boundary tree (ensure adequate spacing)
    - **Alley cropping**: Wide spacing allows crop production between rows while young
    - **Urban forestry**: Excellent park and street tree (plan for massive size)
    - **Reforestation**: Good for restoring degraded dry forest lands

    **Traditional Integration**:
    - Town squares and plazasâ€”iconic shade tree
    - Ranch and farm shadeâ€”provides livestock shelter
    - Roadside plantingâ€”erosion control and shade
    - School and park specimenâ€”multi-generational planning tree

  </AccordionItem>

  <AccordionItem title="Year-Round Care Calendar (Dry Tropical Zone)">
    **December-April (Dry Season/Harvest)**:
    - Peak pod harvest period (December-March)
    - Minimal care requiredâ€”tree thrives in dry conditions
    - Young trees may benefit from monthly deep watering
    - Mature trees need NO intervention
    - Collect and process pods regularly

    **May-June (Flowering/Early Rains)**:
    - Tree flowers as rains arrive
    - New leaf flush begins
    - No special care needed
    - Natural rainfall sufficient
    - Pods from previous season finishing harvest

    **July-October (Rainy Season)**:
    - Pods developing for next dry season harvest
    - Active growth period
    - No watering neededâ€”natural rainfall more than adequate
    - Optional: Apply compost mulch if desired (not necessary)

    **November (Late Rainy/Transition)**:
    - Tree preparing for dry season
    - Pods beginning to mature
    - No care required
    - Assess tree healthâ€”usually excellent

    **Note**: Tamarind is one of the lowest-maintenance fruit/timber trees. Once established (3-5 years), requires essentially ZERO input. Ideal for planting and forgetting. Rewarding for multi-generational propertiesâ€”grandchildren will harvest from trees you plant today.

  </AccordionItem>
