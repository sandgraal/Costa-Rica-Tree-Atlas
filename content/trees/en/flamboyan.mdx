<Callout type="info" title="The Flame Tree">

  scarlet-orange flowers, creating a sight so dramatic that it has inspired

  to Madagascar where it is now endangered in the wild, the Flamboy√°n has become

<Callout type="info" title="Taxonomic Note">
  _Delonix regia_ was first described by Wenceslas Bojer, a Czech-born botanist
  who worked in Mauritius. The species was later formally published by William
  Jackson Hooker and subsequently transferred to the genus _Delonix_ by
  Constantine Samuel Rafinesque. The genus name comes from Greek _delos_
  (conspicuous) and _onyx_ (claw), referring to the showy, claw-like petals.
  Synonyms include _Poinciana regia_, named after Philippe de Longvilliers de
  Poincy, a French governor of the Caribbean island of Saint Kitts who
  introduced the tree to the New World.
</Callout>

## Physical Description

### General Form

The Flamboy√°n is a medium-sized deciduous tree famous for its spectacular umbrella-shaped crown that typically spreads wider than the tree is tall. The spreading, nearly horizontal branches create a magnificent canopy that provides excellent shade for most of the year and transforms into a fiery display during the flowering season.

### Distinctive Features

<TwoColumn>
<Column>

#### Trunk and Bark

- **Trunk**: Often buttressed at base
- **Bark**: Light brown to grayish
- **Texture**: Smooth with prominent lenticels
- **Branches**: Long, spreading, nearly horizontal
- **Structure**: Umbrella-shaped crown

#### Leaves

- **Type**: Bipinnate (doubly compound)
- **Length**: 30-50 cm total
- **Pinnae**: 20-40 pairs per leaf
- **Leaflets**: 10-20 pairs per pinna
- **Size**: 4-12 mm each leaflet
- **Color**: Bright green, feathery appearance
- **Behavior**: Deciduous in dry season

</Column>
<Column>

#### Flowers

- **Color**: Scarlet to orange-red (rarely yellow)
- **Size**: 8-11 cm diameter
- **Petals**: 4 spreading + 1 upright (standard)
- **Standard petal**: Larger, spotted yellow/white
- **Arrangement**: Corymbs at branch ends
- **Season**: Dry to early wet season (Mar-Jun in CR)
- **Display**: Covers entire crown simultaneously

#### Fruits and Seeds

- **Type**: Large woody legume pod
- **Size**: Up to 60 cm long, 5 cm wide
- **Color**: Green when young, dark brown when mature
- **Seeds**: Small, hard, 0.4 g each
- **Persistence**: Pods hang on tree for months

</Column>
</TwoColumn>

<Callout type="tip" title="Field Identification Tip">
  The Flamboy√°n is unmistakable when in flower‚Äîlook for the spectacular umbrella
  crown covered in brilliant red-orange blooms. Out of flowering season,
  identify by the **feathery bipinnate leaves** (fern-like appearance),
  **spreading horizontal branches**, and **large woody seed pods** that often
  persist on the tree. The distinctive umbrella shape with a crown wider than
  the tree is tall is characteristic even from a distance.
</Callout>

## Distribution and Habitat

### Native Range

<DistributionMap countries={["Madagascar"]} />

The Flamboy√°n is endemic to Madagascar's dry deciduous forests on the western coast. In its native range, it is now classified as **Vulnerable** due to habitat loss from charcoal production. Ironically, while endangered in Madagascar, it is one of the most widely cultivated ornamental trees in the tropical world.

### Distribution in Costa Rica

As an introduced ornamental, the Flamboy√°n is cultivated throughout Costa Rica's lowland and mid-elevation areas. It thrives particularly well in the drier Pacific slope regions where the distinct dry season promotes prolific flowering.

### Conservation Paradox

<Callout type="info" title="Rare in the Wild, Common in Cultivation">
  The Flamboy√°n presents a fascinating conservation paradox. In its native
  Madagascar, it is classified as **Vulnerable** and becoming increasingly rare
  due to deforestation for charcoal production. The species was so rare in the
  wild that its natural origin remained unknown until 1932, when a natural
  colony was finally discovered on Madagascar's west coast.

Yet worldwide, the Flamboy√°n is one of the most commonly planted ornamental
trees in tropical and subtropical regions. This cultivation success means the
species is in no danger of extinction, even as wild populations decline.
Conservation efforts focus on protecting remaining native populations in
Madagascar's dry deciduous forests.

</Callout>

## Cultivation and Propagation

### Growing Flamboy√°n

<TwoColumn>
<Column>

#### Site Requirements

**Climate Needs**

- Full tropical to near-tropical climate
- Distinct dry season promotes best flowering
- Temperature range 14-26¬∞C mean annual
- Tolerates drought once established
- Sensitive to frost

**Soil and Light**

- Prefers sandy, well-drained soils
- Tolerates various soil types except waterlogged
- pH 5.5-6.5 optimal (tolerates 4.5-7.5)
- Requires FULL SUN for abundant flowering
- Flowers poorly in shade or very wet climates

</Column>
<Column>

#### Propagation

**From Seeds (Primary Method)**

1. Collect mature pods (dark brown)
2. Scarify seeds (nick or file seed coat)
3. OR soak in hot water, let cool, then 24 hours warm water
4. Sow in well-draining nursery mix
5. Germination in 5-10 days (up to 90% rate)
6. Seedlings grow rapidly
7. Transplant at 20-25 cm height (3-5 months)
8. First flowers in 4-5 years

**From Cuttings**

- Semi-hardwood cuttings possible
- Slower than seeds (months to root)
- Used for yellow variety to ensure true form

</Column>
</TwoColumn>

### Common Issues

<Accordion>
  <AccordionItem title="Shallow Root System" defaultOpen={true}>
    The Flamboy√°n has a notorious shallow, spreading root system that:
    - Can lift and crack sidewalks and driveways
    - May damage foundations if planted too close to buildings
    - Creates bare ground under the canopy (outcompetes grass)
    - Makes the tree prone to toppling in strong winds

    **Solution**: Plant at least 10-15 meters from any paved surfaces or structures.

  </AccordionItem>

  <AccordionItem title="Wind Damage">
    The wood is soft and brittle, making branches prone to breaking in storms. Combined with the shallow roots, strong winds can uproot entire trees.

    **Solution**: Plant in sheltered locations protected from strong winds. Prune to reduce wind resistance if needed.

  </AccordionItem>

  <AccordionItem title="Poor Flowering">
    In consistently wet climates or shaded conditions, the tree may produce few flowers or none at all.

    **Causes**:
    - Too much rainfall / no dry season
    - Insufficient sunlight
    - Too young (under 4-5 years)
    - Excessive nitrogen fertilization

    **Solution**: Plant only in full sun locations in areas with a distinct dry season. Avoid fertilizing mature trees.

  </AccordionItem>

  <AccordionItem title="Messy Seed Pods">
    The large seed pods (up to 60 cm) can be messy when they fall, littering the ground for months.

    **Solution**: Accept as part of the tree's character, or plant where falling pods won't be problematic. Pods can be collected for crafts.

  </AccordionItem>
</Accordion>

## Identification Guide

### How to Identify Flamboy√°n

<Accordion>
  <AccordionItem title="Key Identification Features" defaultOpen={true}>
    **Definitive Characteristics:**

    1. **Flowers**: Unmistakable brilliant scarlet-orange, 8-11 cm across, covering entire crown
    2. **Crown shape**: Umbrella-shaped, spreading, wider than tall
    3. **Leaves**: Feathery bipinnate (fern-like), bright green
    4. **Seed pods**: Very large (up to 60 cm), flat, woody, dark brown when mature
    5. **Bark**: Light brown with prominent lenticels
    6. **Habit**: Deciduous in dry season

  </AccordionItem>

  <AccordionItem title="Seasonal Recognition">
    **Dry Season (Dec-Apr)**:
    - Tree may be partially or fully leafless
    - Old seed pods often still present
    - New flower buds developing

    **Flowering Season (Mar-Jun)**:
    - Spectacular red-orange flower display
    - Flowers appear before or with new leaves
    - Peak bloom covers entire crown

    **Wet Season (May-Nov)**:
    - Full canopy of feathery green leaves
    - Green seed pods developing
    - No flowers

    **Year-round**:
    - Distinctive umbrella-shaped crown
    - Large woody seed pods often persist

  </AccordionItem>

  <AccordionItem title="Similar Species">
    **Yellow Flamboy√°n** (_Delonix regia_ var. _flavida_)
    - Same species but with yellow flowers
    - Much rarer in cultivation
    - Otherwise identical characteristics

    **African Tulip Tree** (_Spathodea campanulata_)
    - Also has orange-red flowers
    - Flowers are tulip-shaped, not flat
    - Leaves simple pinnate, not bipinnate
    - Flowers in upright clusters, not spread across crown

    **Cassia species**
    - May have yellow to orange flowers
    - Smaller flowers in hanging racemes
    - Different leaf structure

  </AccordionItem>
</Accordion>
