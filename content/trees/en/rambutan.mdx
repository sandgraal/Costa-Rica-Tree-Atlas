# Rambutan

<Callout type="info" title="The Hairy Fruit Tree">
  The **Rambutan** (_Nephelium lappaceum_) is one of the most visually striking
  and delicious tropical fruits in the world. Native to Southeast Asia and
  closely related to lychee and longan, this evergreen tree produces clusters of
  golf ball-sized fruits covered in soft, hair-like spinesâ€”giving rise to its
  name from the Malay word _rambut_ meaning "hair." Beneath this exotic exterior
  lies translucent, grape-like flesh that has made rambutan increasingly popular
  in Costa Rica's humid lowlands, where it has found an ideal second home in the
  Caribbean and Central Valley regions.

  lychee (_Litchi chinensis_), longan (_Dimocarpus longan_), and mamÃ³n chino

## Physical Description

### General Form

The rambutan is an evergreen tree with an open, spreading crown. Wild trees can reach impressive heights of 15-24 meters, but cultivated varieties are typically maintained at 4-7 meters for easier harvesting. The tree has been selected over centuries for compact growth habits that maximize fruit accessibility.

### Distinctive Features

<TwoColumn>
<Column>

#### Trunk and Bark

- **Trunk**: Straight, sometimes buttressed
- **Diameter**: 40-60 cm typical, up to 125 cm
- **Bark**: Grayish-brown, moderately rough
- **Crown**: Open, spreading canopy
- **Branches**: Dense, well-branched

#### Leaves

- **Type**: Alternate, pinnately compound
- **Length**: 14-30 cm total
- **Leaflets**: 3-11 per leaf
- **Leaflet size**: 5-15 cm Ã— 3-10 cm
- **Margin**: Entire (smooth edges)
- **Color**: Dark green, glossy above
- **Texture**: Leathery

</Column>
<Column>

#### Flowers

- **Size**: Very small, 2.5-5 mm
- **Petals**: None (apetalous)
- **Color**: Greenish-white to greenish-yellow
- **Arrangement**: Terminal panicles 15-30 cm wide
- **Fragrance**: Faintly sweet
- **Sex**: Trees may be male, female, or hermaphroditic
- **Pollination**: Bees (especially _Apis_ species)

#### Fruit

- **Type**: Drupe
- **Size**: 3-6 cm long, 3-4 cm wide
- **Color**: Red, rarely orange or yellow
- **Surface**: Covered with soft, fleshy spines ("hairs")
- **Flesh**: Translucent white, sweet, grape-like
- **Seed**: Single, glossy brown, 1-1.3 cm
- **Clusters**: 10-20 fruits per pendant cluster

</Column>
</TwoColumn>

<Callout type="tip" title="Field Identification Tip">
  The rambutan is unmistakable when in fruitâ€”look for the distinctive clusters
  of **bright red fruits covered with soft, hair-like spines**. The fruits hang
  in grape-like bunches from branch tips. When not fruiting, identify by the
  **pinnately compound leaves** with 3-11 glossy, leathery leaflets and the
  **open, spreading crown**. Related to lychee but easily distinguished by the
  hairy (vs. bumpy) fruit surface.

Rambutan is native to Southeast Asia, with the Indonesian region being the center of genetic diversity. It has been cultivated throughout the region for centuries and spread via trade routes to East Africa, the Caribbean, and Central America in the 19th and early 20th centuries.

### Distribution in Costa Rica

Rambutan was introduced to Costa Rica and has found excellent growing conditions in the humid lowlands, particularly the Caribbean slope and lower Central Valley. Costa Rica's rainy season timing produces fruit from August to October, coinciding with global market demand.

## Ecological Significance

### Pollinator Relationships

<TwoColumn>
<Column>

#### Primary Pollinators

Rambutan flowers are highly attractive to bees due to their abundant nectar production:

- **Honey bees** (_Apis dorsata_, _A. cerana_): Primary pollinators
- **Stingless bees** (_Trigona_ spp.): Important in Southeast Asia
- **Various flies** (Diptera): Secondary pollinators
- **Ants** (_Solenopsis_ spp.): Incidental pollinators

Female flowers produce 2-3Ã— more nectar than male flowers, attracting more pollinator visits.

</Column>
<Column>

#### Pollination Requirements

- **Cross-pollination** is essential for most cultivars
- Commercial varieties are hermaphroditic but require pollinators
- Only ~1% of female flowers set fruit under natural conditions
- Hand pollination can achieve 13% fruit set
- Adequate bee populations crucial for commercial production
- In Costa Rica, native stingless bees supplement honey bees

## Cultural and Economic Importance

### Global Significance

<TwoColumn>
<Column>

#### Economic Importance

Rambutan is one of the most important tropical fruits commercially:

- **Thailand**: Largest producer (~450,000 tonnes/year)
- **Indonesia**: Second largest (~100,000 tonnes/year)
- **Malaysia**: Third (~60,000 tonnes/year)
- **Costa Rica**: Growing export industry
- **Vietnam, Philippines**: Significant production

The fruit is primarily sold fresh due to its short shelf life, though canned rambutan is exported globally.

</Column>
<Column>

#### Cultural Significance

In Southeast Asia, rambutan has deep cultural roots:

- **Thailand**: Annual Rambutan Fair in Surat Thani (August)
- **Indonesia**: Traditional garden fruit since pre-colonial era
- **Malaysia**: Festival fruit, symbol of abundance
- **Vietnam**: Associated with celebration and hospitality
- **Sri Lanka**: Traditional home garden tree

In Costa Rica, rambutan represents the growing "exotic fruit" industry that diversifies agricultural exports.

</Column>
</TwoColumn>

<Callout type="info" title="Conservation Context">
  Unlike many tropical fruit trees, rambutan is not threatened in its native
  range. It remains common in primary and secondary forests throughout Southeast
  Asia while also being extensively cultivated. The species' adaptability and
  commercial value have ensured its persistence. However, wild genetic diversity
  should be preserved as cultivated varieties represent only a fraction of the
  species' genetic potential.

## Cultivation and Propagation

### Growing Rambutan

<TwoColumn>
<Column>

#### Site Requirements

**Climate Needs**

- Humid tropical lowlands only
- Year-round warmth (21-35Â°C optimal)
- Extremely frost-sensitive
- High humidity essential
- 2,000-3,000 mm rainfall preferred

**Soil and Light**

- Clay loam rich in organic matter
- Acid conditions (pH 5.0-6.5)
- Good drainage but moisture retention
- Full sun for mature trees
- Wind protection important

</Column>
<Column>

#### Propagation

**From Seed (not recommended for production)**

- Seeds viable only fresh (days)
- Wash seeds, sow immediately
- Germination in 7-20 days
- Seedlings grow rapidly
- First fruit in 6-8 years
- Unpredictable fruit quality

**Vegetative (preferred)**

- **Grafting**: Most common commercial method
- **Air-layering**: Traditional method
- **Budding**: For specific cultivars
- Grafted trees fruit in 2-3 years
- Ensures consistent fruit quality

### Common Problems

<Accordion>
  <AccordionItem title="Climate Limitations" defaultOpen={true}>
    Rambutan is strictly a humid tropical lowland tree:
    - **Frost**: Any frost kills the tree; young growth damaged at 4Â°C
    - **Altitude**: Production drops significantly above 600m
    - **Dry periods**: More than 2-3 dry months reduces yields
    - **Wind**: Damages branches and knocks off developing fruit

    **Solution**: Only plant in suitable humid lowland locations with wind protection.

  </AccordionItem>

  <AccordionItem title="Short Shelf Life">
    Fresh rambutan fruit deteriorates rapidly:
    - Shelf life only a few days after harvest
    - Spines and skin turn black quickly
    - Weight loss from transpiration
    - Fruit doesn't ripen after picking

    **Solution**: Harvest at optimal ripeness, keep moist and shaded, transport quickly to market.

  </AccordionItem>

  <AccordionItem title="Pollination Issues">
    Poor fruit set is common:
    - Only ~1% of female flowers naturally set fruit
    - Requires cross-pollination
    - Inadequate bee populations reduce yields
    - Weather affects pollinator activity

    **Solution**: Maintain healthy bee populations, consider managed hives during flowering.

  </AccordionItem>

  <AccordionItem title="Long Juvenile Period">
    Seedling trees take years to fruit:
    - 6-8 years from seed to first fruit
    - Unpredictable fruit quality from seeds
    - Long wait for return on investment

    **Solution**: Use grafted or air-layered plants which fruit in 2-3 years with guaranteed quality.

  </AccordionItem>
</Accordion>

## Identification Guide

### How to Identify Rambutan

<Accordion>
  <AccordionItem title="Key Identification Features" defaultOpen={true}>
    **Definitive Characteristics:**

    1. **Fruit**: Unmistakable bright red (rarely yellow/orange) with soft, flexible "hair-like" spines
    2. **Leaves**: Pinnately compound with 3-11 glossy, leathery leaflets
    3. **Fruit flesh**: Translucent white/pale pink, surrounds single seed
    4. **Fruit clusters**: Pendant bunches of 10-20 fruits
    5. **Tree form**: Open, spreading crown; evergreen

  </AccordionItem>

  <AccordionItem title="Seasonal Recognition">
    **Dry Season (Costa Rica: Dec-Apr)**:
    - Tree remains green (evergreen)
    - Flowering may occur (Jan-Apr)
    - Small greenish flowers in terminal panicles

    **Fruit Season (Aug-Oct in Costa Rica)**:
    - Spectacular fruit clusters visible
    - Bright red/pink color
    - Distinctive "hairy" appearance
    - Peak harvest time

    **Year-round**:
    - Evergreen foliage
    - Compound leaves with glossy leaflets
    - Open crown structure

  </AccordionItem>

  <AccordionItem title="Similar Species">
    **Lychee** (_Litchi chinensis_)
    - Closely related, similar tree form
    - Fruit has bumpy (not hairy) skin
    - Fruit smaller, more pink
    - Flesh similar but sweeter

    **Longan** (_Dimocarpus longan_)
    - Related species
    - Fruit smooth, tan/brown
    - No spines or bumps
    - Smaller fruit, similar flesh

    **MamÃ³n Chino** (_Melicoccus bijugatus_)
    - Same family (Sapindaceae)
    - Smooth green fruit
    - Different leaf structure
    - Native to Americas

  </AccordionItem>
</Accordion>
