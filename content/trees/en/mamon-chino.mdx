### Identification Features

<TwoColumn>
<Column>

#### Bark and Trunk

- **Bark color**: Gray-brown
- **Texture**: Smooth to slightly rough
- **Trunk**: Straight, well-formed
- **Branching**: Dense, spreading

#### Leaves

- **Type**: Compound, pinnate
- **Leaflets**: 2-4 pairs (4-8 total)
- **Size**: Leaflets 7-20 cm long
- **Shape**: Elliptic to oblong
- **Color**: Dark green, glossy
- **New growth**: Reddish, attractive

</Column>
<Column>

#### Flowers

- **Size**: Small (2-5 mm)
- **Color**: Greenish-white
- **Arrangement**: Terminal and axillary panicles
- **Fragrance**: Light, sweet
- **Types**: Male, female, and hermaphrodite on same tree

#### Fruit (DISTINCTIVE!)

- **Shape**: Oval to round
- **Size**: 3-6 cm diameter
- **Skin**: Red or yellow with soft, hair-like spines
- **Spines**: 1-2 cm long, flexible (not sharp!)
- **Flesh**: Translucent white, grape-like
- **Seed**: Large, central, brown
- **Taste**: Sweet, slightly acidic, fragrant

</Column>
</TwoColumn>

<Callout type="tip" title="How to Eat Mam贸n Chino">
  **Opening the fruit**:
  1. Hold fruit firmly
  2. Make a shallow cut around the middle (bite or use thumbnail)
  3. Twist the two halves apart
  4. Pop out the white flesh
  5. Eat around the central seed
  6. Don't eat the seed!

**Tips**:

- The "hairs" are soft, not prickly
- Fresh is besteaten within days of harvest
- Refrigerate to extend freshness
- The seed adheres to flesh in some varieties more than others

</Callout>

## History in Costa Rica

<FeatureBox title="How Rambutan Came to Costa Rica" icon="">
  **Timeline of Introduction**:

- **Early 1900s**: First trees brought to Costa Rica
- **1909-1913**: German immigrants plant rambutan in Lim贸n Province
- **Mid-20th century**: Trees spread through Caribbean lowlands
- **1970s-80s**: Commercial production begins
- **1990s-present**: Costa Rica becomes major Western Hemisphere producer

**Why It Thrived**:
The Caribbean lowlands of Lim贸n Province offer conditions remarkably similar to the rambutan's Southeast Asian homeland:

- High rainfall (3000-4000 mm/year)
- Warm temperatures year-round
- High humidity
- Rich volcanic soils

Today, Costa Rica is the largest rambutan producer in the Americas!

</FeatureBox>

## Cultivation

### Growing Mam贸n Chino in Costa Rica

<Accordion>
  <AccordionItem title="Site Selection">
    **Ideal Conditions**:
    - Caribbean lowland climate
    - High rainfall, no dry season
    - Deep, fertile soil
    - Protected from strong winds
    - Full sun to partial shade

    **Best regions**: Lim贸n Province, Sarapiqu铆, Northern Zone

    **Challenges in other areas**:
    - Pacific side: dry season stress
    - Central Valley: too cool at higher elevations

  </AccordionItem>

  <AccordionItem title="Propagation">
    **Budding/Grafting** (commercial):
    - Ensures variety and quality
    - Fruiting in 2-4 years
    - Use vigorous seedling rootstock
    - Patch or chip budding common

    **Air Layering**:
    - Produces true-to-type trees
    - Slower than grafting
    - 3-4 years to fruit

    **From Seed**:
    - Used for rootstock only
    - Variable fruit quality
    - 5-8 years to fruit
    - Unknown sex until flowering

  </AccordionItem>

  <AccordionItem title="Orchard Management">
    **Spacing**: 10-12 meters between trees

    **Irrigation**: Essential in areas with dry periods

    **Fertilization**: Regular NPK applications, especially during fruiting

    **Pruning**: Shape young trees; maintain accessible canopy height

    **Pest management**:
    - Fruit flies (major pest)
    - Bagging fruit common practice
    - Postharvest treatment for export

  </AccordionItem>
</Accordion>
