## Physical Description

### Overall Form

Copey is a highly adaptable tree that can grow as an independent tree, a strangler (hemi-epiphyte), or even on rocks. It has a dense, rounded crown with distinctive thick, leathery leaves. The branches often produce aerial roots, and the trunk can become massive and irregular when the tree starts as an epiphyte and eventually strangles its host.

## The Autograph Feature

### Nature's Notepad

<Callout type="info" title="Inscribable Leaves">
  The thick, waxy cuticle of Copey leaves allows them to be "inscribed" with a
  fingernail or pointed object. The scratched area turns brown and remains
  visible as the leaf grows, persisting for months or even years. This led to
  its common name "Autograph Tree" and has been used for everything from love
  messages to botanical labels.
</Callout>

<TwoColumn>
<Column>

#### How It Works

- Thick waxy cuticle on leaf surface
- Scratching damages outer cell layers
- Damaged cells die and turn brown
- Marks expand slightly as leaf grows
- Messages persist until leaf falls
- Works best on younger leaves

</Column>
<Column>

#### Historical Uses

- Sailors' messages in Caribbean
- Love letters between couples
- Botanical specimen labels
- Natural curiosity and games
- Educational demonstrations
- Tourist attraction feature

</Column>
</TwoColumn>

## Hemi-Epiphytic Lifestyle

### Starting Life in the Canopy

<Callout type="success" title="Multiple Life Strategies">
  Copey exemplifies ecological flexibility. It can grow as a normal tree from
  the ground, OR start life as an epiphyte when birds deposit seeds in tree
  branches. In the epiphytic form, it sends roots down to the ground and can
  eventually strangle and replace its host treeâ€”similar to strangler figs.
</Callout>

<TwoColumn>
<Column>

#### Epiphytic Start

1. Bird deposits seed in tree branch
2. Seedling establishes on host
3. Roots grow down toward ground
4. Plant becomes more independent
5. May eventually strangle host
6. Stands alone when host dies

</Column>
<Column>

#### Why This Strategy?

- Access to light above forest floor
- Escape ground-level competition
- Birds disperse seeds widely
- Can colonize diverse habitats
- Resilient to disturbance
- Opportunistic establishment

</Column>
</TwoColumn>

## Distribution in Costa Rica

<Callout type="info" title="Where to Find It">
  Copey is remarkably versatile in Costa Rica, found from beach dunes to cloud
  forests. It's common in both Caribbean and Pacific lowlands, abundant in
  middle elevations, and reaches into premontane forests. You'll see it as an
  ornamental tree in gardens, wild in forests, and naturalized along roadsides.
</Callout>

### Resin-Collecting Bees

<Callout type="info" title="Unique Pollination">
  Copey flowers are unusual in offering resin rather than nectar as a reward.
  Stingless bees (Meliponini) and other specialized bees collect this resin to
  build their nests. This creates a unique pollination relationship where bees
  visit for construction materials rather than food.
</Callout>

## Interesting Facts

<Accordion>
<AccordionItem title="âœï¸ Historical Autographs">

The autograph tree has a documented history of use for messages. Caribbean
sailors reportedly wrote on leaves to leave messages for ships that followed.
During the colonial era, botanists used the leaves to label herbarium
specimens. The tradition continues today with tourists and locals inscribing
messages.

</AccordionItem>
<AccordionItem title="ðŸŒ¿ CAM Photosynthesis">

Copey is one of the few trees that can switch to CAM (Crassulacean Acid
Metabolism) photosynthesis during drought stress. This water-conserving
strategyâ€”usually found in succulentsâ€”allows the tree to survive in dry
coastal environments by opening leaf pores only at night.

</AccordionItem>
<AccordionItem title="ðŸ¯ Pitch Apple">

One common name, "Pitch Apple," refers to the sticky resin the tree produces.
Indigenous peoples and early settlers used this resin to waterproof boats,
make torches, and seal containers. The fruit superficially resembles an apple
but is not palatable to humans.

</AccordionItem>
<AccordionItem title="ðŸŒ´ Strangler Behavior">

When Copey starts life as an epiphyte, it can display "strangler" behavior
similar to strangler figs. Its roots encircle the host tree, eventually killing
it. The Copey then stands as an independent tree with a hollow center where
the host rotted away.

</AccordionItem>
</Accordion>
