<Callout type="success" title="The Stranglers That Feed the Forest">

  seeds high in the forest canopy. They send roots cascading earthward,
  eventually enveloping their host tree in a woody embrace that slowly strangles
  it to death. Yet despite this seemingly sinister behavior, strangler figs are
  among the **most important trees in tropical ecosystems**â€”their figs feed
  hundreds of species of birds, mammals, and insects, sustaining wildlife

## The Strangling Life Cycle

<FeatureBox title="How Stranglers Work" icon="ðŸŒ±">
  The strangler fig life cycle is one of nature's most dramatic: **Stage 1:
  Aerial Beginning** - Bird or bat eats fig elsewhere - Deposits seed (with
  fertilizer!) on branch high in canopy - Seed germinates as EPIPHYTE on host
  tree - Young fig is harmlessâ€”just a small plant **Stage 2: The Roots Descend**
  - Fig sends aerial roots down toward ground - Roots can drop 20-30+ meters! -
  Once they reach soil, growth accelerates - More and more roots follow **Stage
  3: The Embrace** - Roots anastomose (fuse together) - Form lattice around host
  trunk - Crown expands, shading host - Roots compete for water and nutrients
  **Stage 4: The Slow Kill** - Host tree gradually weakens - Dies over years to
  decades - Host rots away inside lattice - Fig becomes FREE-STANDING with
  hollow trunk **The Result**: - Hollow lattice trunk (climbable!) - Fig
  occupies former host's canopy position - New "tree" made of fused roots - Host
  completely absorbed This process takes 50-100+ years!
</FeatureBox>

<Callout type="warning" title="Not Always Deadly">
  Not every fig encounter kills the host: **Sometimes hosts survive**: - Host
  may be too large/vigorous - Fig may grow alongside rather than around - Some
  host species are more resistant **Not all figs strangle**: - Some Costa Rican
  Ficus are "normal" trees - Start from seeds on ground - Never develop
  strangling behavior - "Free-standing" figs The strangler strategy is
  remarkable but not universal!
</Callout>

## Ecological Importance

### The Keystone Species

<Callout type="success" title="Why Figs Are Keystones">
  Strangler figs are considered **keystone species**â€”their removal would cause
  cascading ecosystem collapse: **Food Timing**: - Figs fruit YEAR-ROUND - Not
  synchronized to season - Individual trees fruit at different times - Provide
  food when everything else is scarce **The Numbers**: - 70+ bird species eat
  figs in Costa Rica - 30+ mammal species depend on figs - Countless insects
  involved - Fish eat fallen figs! **Nutritional Value**: - High in calcium -
  Good sugar content - Soft, easily digested - Seeds pass through animals **The
  Test**: - Remove figs from ecosystem - Many species would starve - Especially
  in dry season/lean times - Cascading effects throughout food web No other tree
  genus provides such consistent, year-round food for such diversity of
  wildlife!
</Callout>

### The Fig Wasp Mutualism

<FeatureBox title="Figs and Their Wasps" icon="ðŸ">
  Every Ficus species has its own specific pollinating wasp: **The
  Relationship**: - Each fig species = one wasp species - Wasp enters fig
  through tiny pore (ostiole) - Pollinates flowers INSIDE the fig - Lays eggs
  inside some flowers - Wasp offspring develop inside fig - New wasps emerge,
  carrying pollen to next fig **The Dependency**: - Figs CANNOT reproduce
  without their wasp - Wasps CANNOT reproduce without their fig - Obligate
  mutualismâ€”neither survives alone - Co-evolved over millions of years
  **Conservation Implication**: - Save the fig, save the wasp - Save the wasp,
  save the fig - Lose either, lose both - Plus all the species that depend on
  figs! This is one of the most intimate plant-animal relationships in nature!
</FeatureBox>

## Ecological Management & Landscape Use

Strangler figs are not cultivated in the conventional sense â€” they establish naturally wherever their fig-wasp pollinators and frugivorous seed dispersers co-exist. However, land managers increasingly recognise their value and manage landscapes to support them.

### Establishing Figs in Restoration Sites

- **Natural recruitment**: The most effective strategy is to provide host trees and let birds and bats do the planting. Retain tall isolated trees in pastures â€” these become seed-deposition sites
- **Assisted planting**: Collect ripe figs from mature Matapalo trees during fruiting events; mash fruit pulp onto rough bark crevices or epiphyte mats on target host trees at 3â€“5 m height. This mimics natural bird-deposited seed
- **Nursery propagation**: Sow tiny seeds on moist sphagnum moss under 50% shade; keep consistently moist. Germination is rapid (5â€“10 days) but seedlings are extremely delicate. Transplant onto host trees or onto rocks/walls at 6â€“8 weeks
- **Air-layering**: Take 20â€“30 cm cuttings from aerial roots; pot in moist, well-drained mix; roots develop in 4â€“8 weeks. This produces a ground-rooted fig (free-standing form) rather than an epiphytic strangler

- **Nurse trees for host establishment**: Plant fast-growing hosts (Cecropia, Erythrina) in restoration sites; figs will colonise naturally within 5â€“10 years

### Growth Timeline

- **Year 1**: Inconspicuous epiphytic seedling on host tree; 10â€“20 cm
- **Year 5â€“10**: Aerial roots reach the ground; rapid growth once independently rooted
- **Year 15â€“30**: Lattice of roots envelops host trunk; crown merges with or overtops host canopy
- **Year 50â€“100**: Full free-standing structure; hollow trunk forms as host decays; keystone fruiting status achieved
- **Year 200+**: Ancient figs become landscape landmarks; some measured at 30+ m crown diameter
