## Physical Description

### Overall Form

Pejibaye is a clump-forming palm that produces multiple stems from the base. Its most distinctive feature is the densely spiny trunkâ€”sharp black spines that ring the trunk and make harvesting challenging without long poles. The fruits grow in large, heavy clusters just below the crown of feathery fronds.

## Pejibaye Season in Costa Rica

### A Cultural Tradition

<Callout type="info" title="Seasonal Celebration">
  Pejibaye season (April-September, peaking May-July) is eagerly anticipated in
  Costa Rica. Street vendors sell boiled pejibaye by the bag, often with
  mayonnaise for dippingâ€”a beloved local tradition. The fruits must be cooked
  before eating as they contain calcium oxalate crystals that irritate the
  throat when raw.
</Callout>

<TwoColumn>
<Column>

#### How to Eat Pejibaye

1. **Boil** in salted water for 30-60 minutes
2. **Peel** off the thin skin
3. **Remove** the seed
4. **Dip** in mayonnaise (traditional)
5. **Season** with salt if desired
6. **Enjoy** the starchy, nutty flavor

</Column>

- High in beta-carotene (vitamin A)

- Provides vitamin C

## Interesting Facts

<Accordion>
<AccordionItem title="ðŸŒ´ Only Domesticated American Palm">

Of the hundreds of palm species native to the Americas, pejibaye is the only
one fully domesticated for its fruit. This process began over 10,000 years ago
in the Amazon basin, making it one of the oldest cultivated plants in the New
Worldâ€”predating corn and beans as a staple crop.

</AccordionItem>
<AccordionItem title="âš”ï¸ Traditional Weapons">

The extremely hard, dense wood of pejibaye trunks was prized for making
hunting weapons. Indigenous peoples crafted bows, arrows, and spears from it.
The wood is so hard that it can barely be nailedâ€”drilling is required to
avoid splitting.

</AccordionItem>
<AccordionItem title="ðŸ³ Can't Eat It Raw">

Raw pejibaye contains calcium oxalate crystals that cause intense throat
irritation. These compounds are destroyed by cooking, which is why the fruits
are always boiled before eating. The cooking time (30-60 minutes) also makes
the starchy flesh digestible and brings out the distinctive chestnut-like
flavor.

</AccordionItem>
<AccordionItem title="ðŸŒ± Spineless Dreams">

Plant breeders have worked for decades to develop spineless pejibaye varieties
to make harvest easier. While some progress has been made, most commercial
varieties still have spines. The traditional harvest method uses very long
poles with hooks to cut fruit clusters from the groundâ€”avoiding the dangerous
climb up the spiny trunk.

</AccordionItem>
</Accordion>
