# Guayac√°n Real (Lignum Vitae - The Heaviest Wood)

<Callout type="warning" title="CITES Protected - Endangered Species">

  commercial wood**‚Äîso heavy it **sinks in water** and so hard it's

  evergreen foliage, the Guayac√°n Real is both a botanical treasure and a

## Physical Description

### Overall Form

The Guayac√°n Real is a small to medium-sized evergreen tree with a short, often crooked trunk and a dense, rounded crown. It grows extremely slowly‚Äîtaking decades to reach modest size‚Äîbut can live for centuries. The bark is pale and scaly, and the wood within is among the hardest and densest on Earth, with a beautiful greenish-brown heartwood.

## The Extraordinary Wood

### Why Lignum Vitae Is Unique

<FeatureBox title="The World's Hardest Commercial Wood" icon="üíé">
  Lignum vitae wood has properties unlike any other: **Physical Properties**: -
  **Density**: 1.1-1.4 g/cm¬≥ (denser than water‚Äîsinks!) - **Janka Hardness**:
  4,500 lbf (one of highest known) - **Natural oils**: 25-30% by weight (guaiac
  resin) - **Self-lubricating**: The oils make it naturally slippery **Unique
  Characteristics**: - Wood sinks in water immediately - Can be used without
  additional lubrication - Extremely resistant to abrasion - Practically
  impervious to rot - Will not crack or split easily **The Trade-off**: These
  remarkable properties come from extremely slow growth‚Äî it takes 100+ years to
  produce usable timber, making sustainable harvest nearly impossible.
</FeatureBox>

### Historic Uses

<Accordion>
  <AccordionItem title="Ship Bearings and Propeller Shafts">
    The most famous use of lignum vitae: **Marine Applications**: - Stern tube
    bearings (propeller shafts) - Block pulleys and sheaves - Deadeyes and other
    hardware **Why It Worked**: - Self-lubricating in water - Harder than brass
    or bronze - Lasted decades without replacement - No additional lubrication
    needed **Historic Significance**: From the 1600s until synthetic materials
    became available, lignum vitae was *essential* for ocean-going vessels. It
    was literally irreplaceable for certain functions.
  </AccordionItem>

  <AccordionItem title="Scientific Instruments">
    Precision applications: - Clock components (self-lubricating, dimensionally
    stable) - Pulleys for scientific equipment - Measuring tools - Telescope
    mounts The Royal Observatory at Greenwich used lignum vitae for precision
    astronomical instruments.
  </AccordionItem>

  <AccordionItem title="Other Applications">
    **Historical**: - Bowling balls (until 1905) - Cricket bails - Croquet
    mallets - Carved objects and art - Police batons **Modern** (limited by
    availability): - Specialty carvings - Collector items - Conservation
    demonstration - Boutique wood turning
  </AccordionItem>
</Accordion>

## Conservation Status

### Why It's Endangered

<Callout type="warning" title="Critical Conservation Concern">
  Guayac√°n Real faces multiple threats: **Historical Exploitation**: - Harvested
  intensively since 1500s - European demand for ship bearings - Medicinal trade
  (syphilis treatment claims) - Woodworking (density = prestige) **Biological
  Vulnerabilities**: - Extremely slow growth (decades to maturity) - Limited
  habitat (dry forest specialist) - Low seed production - Poor regeneration
  **Current Status**: - IUCN: Endangered - CITES: Appendix II (regulated trade)
  - Costa Rica: Protected species - Wild populations fragmented and depleted
</Callout>

### Conservation Efforts

<Accordion>
  <AccordionItem title="Legal Protection">
    **International**: - CITES Appendix II (all _Guaiacum_ species) - Export
    requires permits - Commercial trade monitored **Costa Rica**: - Listed as
    threatened species - Protected in national parks - Logging prohibited -
    Restoration programs active
  </AccordionItem>

  <AccordionItem title="Restoration Programs">
    Efforts to restore populations include: - Seed collection from wild trees -
    Nursery propagation programs - Reforestation in protected areas - Community
    conservation education - Research on cultivation techniques The extremely
    slow growth makes restoration challenging‚Äî trees planted today won't mature
    for 50+ years.
  </AccordionItem>
</Accordion>

<FeatureBox title="Historic Medicinal Fame" icon="‚öïÔ∏è">
  Lignum vitae gained its "Wood of Life" name from medicinal claims: **16th-19th

  antibiotics) - **Gout and rheumatism**: Wood and resin decoctions -
  **Respiratory conditions**: Traditional applications - **Fever reduction**:
  Various preparations **The Guaiac Compound**: - Resin contains guaiacol - Has

  was ineffective and potentially harmful. The compound guaiacol has limited
