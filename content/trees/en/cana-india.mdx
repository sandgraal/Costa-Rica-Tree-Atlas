## Physical Description

### Overall Form

Ca√±a India grows as an unbranched or sparingly branched tree-like plant with a distinctive rosette of long, strap-shaped leaves at the top. The thick, woody stem stores water, and the plant can reach impressive heights over many years. Multiple trunks often grow together when planted as cuttings, creating the characteristic living fence appearance.

### Propagation

<Callout type="success" title="Easiest Plant to Propagate">
  Ca√±a India may be the easiest plant in the world to propagate. Simply cut a

  This ease of propagation is why it became so popular for living fences.

## Interesting Facts

<Accordion>
<AccordionItem title="üåç From Africa to Costa Rica">

Despite being called "Ca√±a India" (Indian cane), this plant is actually native
to tropical Africa‚Äîfrom Sudan to Mozambique. It was brought to the Americas
during the colonial era, possibly via Asian trade routes, which may explain the
"India" misnomer. It has become so naturalized in Costa Rica that many people
assume it's native.

</AccordionItem>
<AccordionItem title="üåô Night Pollinators">

The intensely fragrant night-blooming flowers evolved to attract hawk moths
(Sphingidae). These large moths hover like hummingbirds while extending their
extremely long tongues to reach nectar. The white color and strong fragrance
are classic moth-pollination adaptations‚Äîmaximizing visibility and detectability
in darkness.

</AccordionItem>
<AccordionItem title="üè† NASA-Certified Air Purifier">

In NASA's famous Clean Air Study, Dracaena fragrans was identified as one of
the most effective plants for removing indoor air pollutants, particularly
formaldehyde, trichloroethylene, and benzene. This research launched the plant
to superstardom in the houseplant world.

</AccordionItem>
<AccordionItem title="üêâ Dragon's Blood Connection">

The genus name "Dracaena" (dragon) comes from the red resin produced by some
species, known as "dragon's blood." While D. fragrans doesn't produce much
resin, its relatives like D. draco were historically important sources of this
valuable red pigment used in medicine, dyes, and varnishes.

</AccordionItem>
</Accordion>
