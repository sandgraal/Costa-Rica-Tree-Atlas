# Cas (Costa Rican Guava)

<Callout type="success" title="Costa Rica's Signature Fruit">
  The **Cas** (_Psidium friedrichsthalianum_), known as Costa Rican Guava or
  Sour Guava, is perhaps the most distinctly Costa Rican fruit tree. While the
  common guava is eaten fresh throughout the tropics, the cas has carved its own
  nicheâ€”its intensely tart, aromatic fruits are transformed into *refresco de
  cas*, one of the most iconic and refreshing beverages in Costa Rican cuisine.
  Found primarily in Costa Rica and Panama's highlands, this small tree
  represents a unique piece of Central American biodiversity and culinary
  heritage. No visit to a Costa Rican *soda* (local restaurant) is complete
  without trying this uniquely tangy, fragrant drink.
</Callout>

## Physical Description

### General Form

The Cas is a small to medium evergreen tree with a dense, rounded canopy. It resembles its cousin the common guava but tends to be smaller and more compact. The tree is often multi-stemmed and well-suited to home gardens and small orchards.

### Identification Features

<TwoColumn>
<Column>

#### Bark and Trunk

- **Bark color**: Smooth, reddish-brown
- **Texture**: Peeling in thin layers
- **Pattern**: Mottled appearance
- **Trunk**: Often multi-stemmed

#### Leaves

- **Type**: Simple, opposite
- **Shape**: Elliptic to oblong
- **Size**: 5-12 cm long
- **Texture**: Leathery
- **Color**: Dark green, lighter beneath
- **Venation**: Prominent parallel veins
- **Scent**: Aromatic when crushed (guava-like)

</Column>
<Column>

#### Flowers

- **Size**: 2-3 cm diameter
- **Color**: White
- **Petals**: 4-5, with numerous stamens
- **Fragrance**: Sweet, typical guava scent
- **Location**: Leaf axils
- **Timing**: Dry season into early rainy season

#### Fruit

- **Shape**: Round to slightly oval
- **Size**: 3-5 cm diameter
- **Skin**: Green, thin, smooth
- **Flesh**: White to cream, granular
- **Seeds**: Numerous, small, hard
- **Taste**: VERY TART (much more than common guava)
- **Aroma**: Intense, fragrant, characteristic

</Column>
</TwoColumn>

<Callout type="tip" title="Identifying Ripe Cas">
  Unlike many fruits, ripe cas: - **Stays green** (doesn't turn yellow) -
  **Softens slightly** when gently pressed - **Develops intense aroma** that's
  unmistakable - **Falls from tree** naturally when fully ripe The intense
  sourness is normalâ€”it's prized for juice, not fresh eating!
</Callout>

**Ingredients**:

- 4-5 ripe cas fruits
- 1 liter cold water
- Sugar to taste (Â½ to 1 cup)

**Preparation**:

1. Wash fruits and cut in half
2. Scoop flesh and seeds into blender
3. Add water and blend thoroughly
4. Strain through fine mesh to remove seeds

- Best served very cold

### Growing Cas

<Accordion>
  <AccordionItem title="Site Requirements">
    **Ideal Conditions**:
    - Elevation 800-1800m (highland species)
    - Cool nights, warm days
    - Full sun
    - Well-drained soil
    - Protected from extreme winds

    **Central Valley Ideal**:
    The Central Valley of Costa Rica (including San JosÃ©, Heredia, Alajuela, Cartago) provides ideal conditions with its cooler climate and fertile volcanic soils.

  </AccordionItem>

  <AccordionItem title="Propagation">
    **From Seed** (common):
    - Fresh seeds have best germination
    - Plant immediately after extracting
    - Germination in 2-4 weeks
    - Variable fruit quality

    **From Cuttings**:
    - Semi-hardwood cuttings work
    - Use rooting hormone
    - Maintains parent characteristics

    **Grafting**:
    - For superior varieties
    - Earlier fruiting
    - Ensures quality

  </AccordionItem>

  <AccordionItem title="Care">
    **Watering**: Regular during establishment, moderate once mature

    **Fertilization**: Balanced fertilizer 3-4 times yearly

    **Pruning**: Shape young trees; remove dead wood

    **Pest management**: Generally few problems; watch for fruit flies

    **Harvest**: When fruits yield slightly to pressure and aroma intensifies

  </AccordionItem>

## Cultural Significance

### In Costa Rican Identity

<FeatureBox title="A Uniquely Costa Rican Fruit" icon="ðŸ‡¨ðŸ‡·">
  The cas holds a special place in Costa Rican culture:

- **National beverage**: Refresco de cas is quintessentially Tico
- **Home gardens**: Found in patios throughout the Central Valley
- **Soda culture**: No traditional restaurant is complete without it
- **Childhood memories**: Many Costa Ricans associate it with home
- **Local pride**: A fruit that's "ours" - not found widely elsewhere

The intense tartness that foreigners find surprising is exactly what Costa Ricans love about it!

</FeatureBox>

### Cultural & Historical Context

<Accordion>
  <AccordionItem title="Pre-Columbian Heritage">
    Indigenous peoples of the Central Valley and highland regions gathered wild cas fruits long before European contact. Archaeological evidence from sites in the ReventazÃ³n and TÃ¡rcoles river basins suggests that *Psidium* species were among the wild fruits regularly consumed by pre-Columbian populations. The fruit's intense sourness made it a natural flavoring agent, and its medicinal properties were recognized in traditional healing practices.
  </AccordionItem>

<AccordionItem title="Colonial & Modern History">
  During the colonial period, cas remained a local fruit of little commercial
  interest to Spanish settlers focused on cacao and coffee. It was the growth of
  Costa Rica's unique _soda_ culture in the 20th century that elevated cas to
  iconic status. As small family-run eateries spread across the country,
  refresco de cas became a staple alongside gallo pinto and casados. Today,
  industrial processing of cas pulp has expanded availability, with frozen pulp
  exported to Costa Rican diaspora communities in the United States.
</AccordionItem>

  <AccordionItem title="Cas in Contemporary Culture">
    In modern Costa Rica, cas has transcended its role as a simple fruit:

    - **Festivals**: Local harvest festivals (_ferias_) in the Central Valley celebrate cas season (Juneâ€“September)
    - **Gastronomy**: Chef-driven restaurants now feature cas in craft cocktails, ceviches, and dessert sauces
    - **Identity marker**: Offering a foreign visitor refresco de cas is a point of cultural pride
    - **Agricultural heritage**: Backyard cas trees are passed down through generations, connecting families to their rural roots
    - **Artisanal products**: Small producers create cas vinegar, cas wine, and cas hot sauce

  </AccordionItem>
</Accordion>

## Conservation Status

<Callout type="info" title="Conservation Notes">
  **Status**: Least Concern (LC)

The cas is not threatened, but its limited natural range makes genetic conservation important. Most trees are cultivated rather than wild. Maintaining diversity in cultivated populations helps preserve this unique species.

</Callout>

## Quick Identification Guide

<FeatureBox title="How to Recognize Cas" icon="ðŸ”">
  1. **Small tree** (5-8 m) similar to common guava 2. **Smooth, peeling bark**
  in reddish-brown patches 3. **Opposite, aromatic leaves** with parallel veins
  4. **Small green fruits** (3-5 cm) that stay green when ripe 5. **Intensely
  fragrant** with characteristic sour-guava aroma 6. **VERY TART taste** - this
  is normal! 7. **Highland distribution** (500-2000m elevation)
