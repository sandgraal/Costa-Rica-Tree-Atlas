## Physical Description

### Overall Form

Cacao is a small, shade-loving understory tree with a distinctive growth pattern. It produces flowers and fruits directly from its trunk and main branches (cauliflory)‚Äîan unusual trait that allows pollinators and harvesters easy access. The large, colorful pods hanging from the trunk make it instantly recognizable.

### Distinctive Features

<TwoColumn>
<Column>

#### Leaves

- **Type**: Simple, alternate
- **Size**: 20-40 cm long
- **Shape**: Oblong-elliptic
- **Color**: New leaves often red/bronze
- **Texture**: Leathery when mature
- **Feature**: Large, pendulous when young

#### Bark

- **Color**: Gray-brown
- **Texture**: Smooth to slightly rough
- **Feature**: Flowers emerge directly from bark
- **Pattern**: Cauliflorous growth

</Column>
<Column>

#### Flowers

- **Size**: Only 1-2 cm diameter
- **Color**: White, pink, or yellowish
- **Location**: Directly on trunk/branches
- **Abundance**: Thousands per tree/year
- **Pollination**: Tiny midges (Ceratopogonidae)
- **Success Rate**: Less than 5% become pods

#### Fruits (Pods)

- **Type**: Berry (botanically)
- **Size**: 15-35 cm long
- **Color**: Yellow, orange, red, or purple
- **Seeds**: 20-50 per pod
- **Pulp**: White, sweet, edible
- **Maturation**: 5-6 months

</Column>
</TwoColumn>

## From Bean to Chocolate

### The Transformation Process

<Callout type="info" title="Complex Chemistry">
  Raw cacao beans taste nothing like chocolate‚Äîthey're bitter and astringent.
  The familiar chocolate flavor only develops through careful fermentation,

  by ancient Mesoamericans thousands of years ago.

## Cacao Varieties

### Main Types Grown in Costa Rica

<TwoColumn>
<Column>

#### Criollo

- Original Mesoamerican variety
- Finest flavor, most aromatic
- Low yield, disease susceptible
- "The aristocrat of cacao"
- Rare (< 5% world production)

#### Forastero

- Most common worldwide (80%+)
- Hardy, high-yielding
- Stronger, more bitter flavor
- Basis of most commercial chocolate
- Originally from Amazon basin

</Column>
<Column>

#### Trinitario

- Hybrid of Criollo √ó Forastero
- Balanced flavor and yield
- Moderately disease resistant
- Common in fine chocolate
- Originated in Trinidad (1700s)

#### Costa Rican Specialty

- Heritage Criollo populations
- Fine-flavor designation
- Growing organic/specialty market
- Tourism chocolate experiences
- Indigenous cultivation traditions

</Column>
</TwoColumn>

## Cultural Significance

### Ancient Mesoamerican Heritage

<Callout type="success" title="Currency and Ceremony">

## Interesting Facts

<Accordion>
<AccordionItem title="ü™∞ Tiny Essential Pollinators">

Cacao flowers are pollinated almost exclusively by tiny midges (Forcipomyia and
related genera) barely 1-3mm long. These insects breed in rotting leaf litter
on the forest floor. Without them, no chocolate! Maintaining leaf litter and
shade in cacao farms is essential for pollinator populations.

</AccordionItem>
<AccordionItem title="üí∞ Ancient Currency">

Cacao beans were so valuable to the Aztecs that they were used as currency.
Records show prices like: 1 turkey = 100 beans, 1 avocado = 3 beans, 1 tamale
= 1 bean. Counterfeiting was a problem‚Äîsome traders filled bean shells with
mud to pass them off as real currency!

</AccordionItem>
<AccordionItem title="üß¨ Genetic Diversity Center">

Recent genetic research suggests the Amazon basin, not Central America, is
cacao's original home. However, Central America‚Äîincluding Costa Rica‚Äîis where
it was first domesticated and where unique genetic varieties developed over
thousands of years of cultivation.

</AccordionItem>
<AccordionItem title="üç´ From Bitter to Sweet">

Ancient Mesoamerican chocolate was nothing like modern candy bars. It was a
bitter, spicy beverage mixed with chili peppers, vanilla, and sometimes corn.
Adding sugar to chocolate only happened after Europeans brought it back to
Spain and combined it with cane sugar‚Äîitself a relatively new crop in Europe.

</AccordionItem>
</Accordion>
